===== POLICY IC/ICA — School Year/Calendar/Instructional Time =====

OVERVIEW
The Board determines the school year length prior to each school year's end, ensuring instructional hours meet state law requirements and support district educational objectives.

EDUCATIONAL PROCESS DEFINED
The Board defines "actively engaged in the educational process" as time when students are working toward achieving educational objectives under the supervision of a licensed teacher. Included activities: classroom instruction and instructional time outside classrooms; individual student work time at school (study hall, library research); individual work in non-classroom environments completing required learning activities and digital research; school-related field trips; independent study; assemblies, performances, exhibitions; work experience related to educational objectives; blended learning and supplemental online education courses (occurring on/off school grounds and outside normal schedules).

Blended learning is defined as a formal education program through which a student learns at least in part through digital content with some element of student control and at least in part at a supervised physical location. Supplemental online courses are taught by a licensed teacher, delivered via a technologically enhanced format, and developed internally or obtained from providers.

Excluded from the educational process: lunch; pre-class and after-school waiting time; teacher preparation time.

Remote learning during emergency closures qualifies as educational process; the District follows Colorado Department of Education guidance.

SUPERVISION OF A LICENSED TEACHER DEFINED
Under the supervision of a teacher means the teacher is designing learning activities for and regulating and monitoring those activities of students. Physical presence is not required; the teacher must exercise direction and control over student activities.

SCHOOL CALENDAR
The superintendent prepares the calendar for Board approval before year-end. Calendar revisions after year-end require appropriate notice. The calendar must contain sufficient instructional days meeting state law requirements while supporting district educational objectives and community/staff needs. Parents/guardians receive calendar copies. Except for emergencies, calendar changes require adequate and timely notice of no less than 30 days.

Adoption/Revision dates: September 24, 2019; September 12, 2023; June 17, 2025.

===== POLICY IGDA — Student Activities and Organizations =====

GENERAL POLICY FRAMEWORK
Poudre School District encourages student participation in activities and organizations, but this shall not be construed to require the District or any of its schools to sponsor any particular activity or organization. The District may allocate resources among activities based on institutional judgment and educational mission priorities.

SCHOOL-SPONSORED ACTIVITIES AND ORGANIZATIONS ESTABLISHMENT AND OVERSIGHT
School-sponsored activities may be established by the principal with superintendent approval. District employees may apply in writing to establish sponsored activities, with principal decisions subject to superintendent review. A District employee must supervise or advise each approved activity.

MEMBERSHIP REQUIREMENTS AND PROTECTIONS
Membership is voluntary and limited to currently enrolled students unless law requires otherwise. Activities may impose academic qualifications for membership where necessarily related to the purposes of the activity or organization. Prohibited discrimination includes denials based on race, color, national origin, religion, ancestry, sex, sexual orientation, or disability. Sex-based restrictions apply only where necessary to preserve the health, safety or welfare of students, or where sex is a bona fide and integral qualification.

RESOURCE ALLOCATION
The principal determines financial support levels and facility access, subject to superintendent approval and policies DJ, DJA, and DJB (purchasing authority).

SECONDARY SCHOOL STUDENT-INITIATED AND LED ORGANIZATIONS — APPROVAL PROCESS
Secondary students may establish organizations upon prior written approval of the school's principal and the superintendent's designee through District-approved applications complying with laws, discipline rules, and policy terms. Annual re-application is required.

EQUAL ACCESS AND NON-DISCRIMINATION
The District shall not deny equal access to school facilities for meetings of approved student-initiated and led organizations, or otherwise discriminate against such organizations based on religious, political, philosophical or other content.

MEETING PARAMETERS
Organizations meet during non-instructional time (before instruction begins or after it ends; lunch periods overlapping instruction count as instructional). Meetings must not materially and substantially interfere with the orderly conduct of educational activities and cannot be regularly attended by nonschool persons. A school employee must monitor meetings, attending religious meetings only in a nonparticipatory capacity.

PUBLICATIONS AND COMMUNICATIONS
Approved organizations appear in yearbooks and publications on the same basis as other noncurriculum-related student groups. They may post on designated bulletin boards using District-approved forms and announce meetings via public address system.

FINANCIAL AND SPONSORSHIP CLARIFICATIONS
Organizations bear all out-of-pocket costs. The District maintains no accounts, funds, or involvement in fundraising. Organizations shall not be deemed to be sponsored or endorsed by the District and cannot represent District sponsorship or control. Organizations may identify student membership but may not represent that they are sponsored, supervised or controlled by the District.

PROHIBITED ACTIVITIES AND COMPLIANCE
Organizations must not engage in activities contrary to law, District policies/regulations or school rules; that disrupt or clearly threaten to disrupt the orderly operation of the District or school; or that would adversely affect the health, safety or welfare of any student or District employee. Noncompliance grounds revocation rights.

RELIGIOUS ACTIVITY PROTECTIONS
Officials may not influence the form or content of any prayer or religious activity, require any person to participate in prayer or religious activity, or compel any school employee to attend any meeting if the meeting is contrary to the beliefs of the employee.

LEGAL REFERENCES
Equal Access Act, 20 U.S.C. 4071-4074; C.R.S. 22-1-117; C.R.S. 22-1-118.

CROSS REFERENCES
KF (Use of District Facilities); DJ, DJA, DJB (Purchasing policies).

===== POLICY IGF-R — Curriculum Review =====

YEAR ONE: MATERIALS ADOPTION — MASTER STUDY, INITIAL STAGE
The curriculum specialist must inform all stakeholders of the initiation of the process and that communication will continue through the study; create a communication plan involving appropriate individuals and groups throughout the process; determine study participants and define their respective roles; select the method for compensating those involved (stipend, release-time, graduate credit, other); and prepare a preliminary timeline.

FOUNDATION STAGE
Conduct foundational work including comprehensive data analysis from preceding years and examination of stakeholder-identified issues. Contact affected parties including curriculum coordinating personnel, principals, community, teachers, and the curriculum study committee. Review research, trends, and best practices. Develop or review philosophy, beliefs, and standards for the curriculum area, aligned with district philosophy and beliefs. Perform internal analysis of current curriculum documenting strengths (qualities enabling standards achievement) and weaknesses (characteristics restricting standards achievement, including gaps and redundancies). Conduct external analysis examining social, political, demographic, economic, and technological impacts on students. Identify critical issues regarding funding, space, support, and maintenance. Assess foundation stage effectiveness.

MATERIALS SELECTION/DEVELOPMENT STAGE
Identify student expectations — state what the student will be able to do as a result of participating in the program. Identify essential components including content and teaching strategies. Determine sequence of material selection activities: survey available materials, consider alternatives, narrow possibilities, conduct on-site trials at every level affected, assess trials, select materials for purchase or development, develop assessment tools, seek approval, and plan staff development. Assess development stage.

YEAR TWO — INITIAL IMPLEMENTATION STAGE
The curriculum coordinator with department chairs and teachers implement review processes: provide staff development for implementation; select implementation approach (pilot in several classrooms for modifications, or move to full district-wide implementation if uncomplicated); refine student expectations; refine assessment tools and gather baseline data on student achievement; assess initial implementation and modify accordingly; identify concern areas; initiate program monitoring procedures; address school-to-career, technology, and diversity infusion.

YEAR THREE
Continue gathering student achievement data; continue developing appropriate assessment strategies; monitor standards through assessment, and modify and support as necessary; continue staff development; continue identifying improvement areas; examine curriculum for strengths and weaknesses; begin data analysis; identify additional needed data; monitor school-to-career, technology, and diversity infusion procedures.

YEAR FOUR
Continue Year Three processes; use assessment data and other data to do program evaluation; share data and data analysis with stakeholders.

YEARS FIVE THROUGH SEVEN
Years Five and Six continue prior activities; Year Six includes communicating with stakeholders about materials selection and researching trends and best practices. Year Seven includes intense review of assessment data, research trends, and current curriculum, and reviewing all standards and benchmarks.

The District establishes targets to reduce achievement gaps in student performance related to ethnicity, gender, disability and limited English proficiency while holding high expectations for all students. The evaluation process uses classroom, schoolwide and Districtwide formative and common summative assessments to inform instructional decisions and program modifications.

===== POLICY IHAAA — School-Sponsored Student Publications =====

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
School-sponsored student publications serve to provide guided instructional experiences in reporting, writing, editing, and understanding English, and responsible journalism. Newspapers additionally disseminate school-related information.

PROHIBITED EXPRESSION
Students are prohibited from publishing: obscene expression; libelous, slanderous, or defamatory expression under state law; false expression regarding any person; expression creating clear and present danger of the commission of unlawful acts or material disruption to school operations; expression violating others' privacy rights; expression violating lawful school regulations maintaining educational environments.

INSTRUCTOR RESPONSIBILITIES
The publications instructor directs the learning experience, teaches responsible expression and professional standards, and may assign work or delegate content decisions to student editors subject to review and approval. Instructors must develop editorial policy guidelines addressing profanity, advertising, confidentiality, privacy, deaths, and letters to the editor, reviewed yearly.

REQUIRED DISCLAIMER
All publications must contain a disclaimer stating student expression is not an expression of the District or of District policy and that the District is immune from liability.

DISTRIBUTION COORDINATION
The principal coordinates time, place, and manner of distribution to minimize instructional conflicts.

REVIEW AND APPEAL PROCEDURES
Staff notifying administrators of prohibited expression triggers an informal hearing where students and instructors present views without legal representation. The principal issues a written decision specifying violated rules. Appeals proceed to the superintendent, then potentially to the Board.

===== POLICY IHBA — Abbreviated School Day =====

DEFINITIONS
Abbreviated School Day encompasses instruction or educational services lasting fewer hours than peers in the same grade and school, whether planned or unplanned. Abbreviated School Day Schedule constitutes a designed and approved plan by the IEP or 504 team for regularly providing fewer instructional hours than majority peers in the same grade and school. Student with a disability includes those determined eligible under IDEA, Section 504, or for whom the District has received evaluation requests under either statute.

PLACEMENT CIRCUMSTANCES
Students with disabilities may be placed on abbreviated schedules when: the IEP or 504 team determines the schedule is necessary to provide free appropriate public education (FAPE) in the least restrictive environment (LRE) based on individualized needs; or the student has met graduation requirements and receives post-secondary transition services. Placement cannot occur based on administrative convenience or a lack of resources, including staffing availability, waiting lists, facilities access, or related services availability.

INITIAL PLACEMENT REQUIREMENTS — PROCEDURAL SAFEGUARDS AND TEAM DETERMINATION
Prior to meetings discussing abbreviated schedules, parents must receive applicable procedural safeguards notices. The IEP or 504 team must determine appropriateness based on unique disability-related needs and document how FAPE will be achieved and whether the schedule impacts least restrictive environment placement.

REQUIRED DOCUMENTED COMPONENTS
The IEP or 504 plan must address: how the schedule supports return to full-day attendance, including stages for gradual reintroduction, unless the team documents that gradual reintroduction is inappropriate due to circumstances like degenerative medical conditions; how the schedule ensures progress toward IEP goals and general education curriculum advancement; specified instructional hours, related services, and percentage of school year on abbreviated schedule; measurement methodology for progress toward IEP goals and benchmarks; expected return date to full-day schedule based on unique needs.

ADDITIONAL REQUIRED DOCUMENTATION
The plan must include: daily school schedule specifications; team justification for the abbreviated schedule; timeline for IEP or 504 team review; appropriate contact point for policy violation concerns; documentation that parents received procedural safeguard information; parent's informed written consent if the abbreviated schedule is disconnected from the FAPE offer.

PRIOR WRITTEN NOTICE REQUIREMENTS
For IEP students, initial placement cannot use the IEP amendment process. Prior written notice must include: supports, services, and alternatives that have been provided or considered before placing the student on abbreviated schedule; outcomes and justification for the abbreviated schedule.

PARENT DISPUTE REMEDIES
When parents disagree that an abbreviated schedule is necessary for FAPE, they may pursue existing remedies: for IEP students, requesting mediation, filing state complaints, or pursuing due process; for 504 students, filing Office for Civil Rights complaints.

ABBREVIATED SCHEDULE NOT CONNECTED TO FAPE
If an abbreviated schedule is not connected to the FAPE offer and is not a permissible disciplinary removal, the school must obtain informed and written consent from the student's parent/guardian prior to implementing the schedule. Parents may revoke consent in writing at any time. Requests must use language and format that is accessible to the parent/guardian.

REVIEW SCHEDULE
The IEP or 504 team must review the abbreviated schedule within 30 calendar days of placement, unless the District and parent agree otherwise at the initial meeting. Subsequent review frequency must be documented in the plan; schedules exceeding 30 calendar days require parent agreement. Regardless of parent agreement, annual review is mandatory. During reviews, teams must discuss progress toward IEP goals and stages described by the team for gradual reintroduction. If expected progress is not occurring, the team must consider adjusting the supports and services provided to ensure progress.

ENROLLMENT, SCHEDULE, ATTENDANCE, AND PARTICIPATION
High school students with disabilities voluntarily enrolling in reduced class loads or part-time status consistent with nondisabled peers, or those whose parents voluntarily enrolled them part-time (including dually enrolled students), are not considered on abbreviated schedules. Students placed on abbreviated schedules may not be considered truant or chronically absent based solely on the Abbreviated School Day Schedule. Students on abbreviated schedules should not be determined ineligible to participate in field trips, school functions, and extracurricular activities based solely on the Abbreviated School Day Schedule.

SCHOOL DISCIPLINE AND ABBREVIATED DAYS
When schools shorten a student's day due to behavioral concerns, this must be counted as a disciplinary removal, regardless of whether the student is formally suspended. This includes: requesting parent pickup before the school day ends due to behavior; teachers excluding students from class due to behavior; administrators unilaterally determining abbreviated attendance due to behavior. These requirements apply even when parents agreed to early pickup due to behavioral concerns, but do not apply when parents independently requested early pickup. Partial disciplinary removals must be accurately recorded and tracked to ensure the student with a disability receives the disciplinary protections under IDEA or Section 504. Nothing prohibits schools from disciplining students for conduct violations, provided discipline complies with District policy and IDEA or Section 504 protections.

LEGAL REFERENCES
20 U.S.C. 1401 et seq. (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act); 29 U.S.C. 701 et seq. (Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973); C.R.S. 22-20-123 (Abbreviated School Days); 1 CCR 301-8 (Rules for the Administration of the Exceptional Children's Educational Act).

No provisions regarding grade point averages, class ranking, automated decision systems, algorithms, artificial intelligence, machine learning, or predictive tools appear in this policy.

===== POLICY IHBG — Home-Based Education =====

NOTIFICATION AND ENROLLMENT REQUIREMENTS
Parents/guardians must submit written notification of the establishment of the program to the assistant director of student outreach 14 days prior to starting. Annual renotification is required if the program continues. Written certification must include the name, age, place of residence and number of hours of attendance of each child enrolled. These requirements apply only to children ages 6-15.

PRIOR TRUANCY RESTRICTION
Students with habitual truancy in the six months before proposed home-based enrollment face an additional barrier. Such students cannot enroll unless parents first submit a written description of the curriculum to be used in the program along with standard notification.

RECORD MAINTENANCE
Parents must maintain permanent records including attendance data, test and evaluation results and immunization records. Records must be produced to the assistant director upon 14 days' written notice if the superintendent has probable cause of non-compliance.

ASSESSMENT REQUIREMENTS AT SPECIFIED GRADES
Each child participating in the program shall be evaluated when he or she reaches grades three, five, seven, nine and eleven through either nationally standardized achievement testing or evaluation by a qualified person as defined by law. Results must be submitted to the district or to an independent/parochial school (with school name reported to the district).

STANDARDIZED TEST SCORE THRESHOLDS
Children scoring above the 13th percentile shall continue to be exempt from compulsory school attendance. Scores at or below the 13th percentile allow parents to request retesting using alternate approved tests.

QUALIFIED EVALUATOR STANDARD
Evaluations by qualified persons showing sufficient academic progress according to his or her ability allow continued exemption from compulsory attendance.

REMEDIATION REQUIREMENT
If retest scores remain at or below the 13th percentile or qualified evaluation shows insufficient progress, the District shall require the parent/guardian to enroll the child in a public, independent or parochial school until the next testing period.

PARTIAL ENROLLMENT OPTIONS
Home-based students may: enroll in PSD Options School; enroll in District school classes on space-available basis if generating at least 0.5 FTE per-pupil funding; or remain exclusively in home-based education.

INSTRUCTIONAL MATERIALS ACCESS
Access to District textbooks and materials is limited based on enrollment: Options School enrollees access the lending library only; District class enrollees access materials for enrolled classes only; exclusively home-based students receive no District material access.

EXTRACURRICULAR PARTICIPATION
A child in a nonpublic home-based educational program may participate in District extracurricular and interscholastic activities as provided by law and District policy.

LEGAL REFERENCES
C.R.S. 22-33-104.5; C.R.S. 22-33-107.

Note: standardized test percentile scores (13th percentile threshold) are used as a scored cutoff determining continued exemption from compulsory attendance versus mandated enrollment in a public, independent, or parochial school; no automated/algorithmic/AI system is mentioned — the process relies on standardized test scoring and human evaluator judgment.

===== POLICY IJL — Library Materials Selection and Adoptions =====

PURPOSE AND FOUNDATIONAL PRINCIPLES
The Poudre School District R-1 Library Media Technology Centers provide resources in multiple formats to support curriculum and meet student and staff educational needs. The district adopts the American Library Association's Library Bill of Rights and the American Association of School Librarians' School Library Bill of Rights for School Library Media Programs as guiding philosophies.

SELECTION CRITERIA
Media selection shall address six key objectives: implementing and enriching the curriculum; helping students develop information access and usage skills; helping students develop personal codes of conduct; stimulating imagination, creativity, and vision through individual inquiry; encouraging critical thinking; providing pleasure during leisure time.

SELECTION AUTHORITY AND PROCESS
Building principals delegate material selection responsibility to licensed media specialists. While teachers, administrators, students, and site teams may recommend materials, the responsibility for the selection decision will rest with the media specialist applying professional collection development standards.

CONTENT CONSIDERATIONS
Materials addressing controversial subjects may be included when language, sexual content, or violence are integral to plot development or accurate reporting, provided such treatment avoids purposefully or sensationally exploited presentation.

VIDEO RATING GUIDELINES
PG-13 videos require no curricular justification for viewers 13+; R-rated content requires curricular objectives and parental permission slips for students under 17. R-rated materials are prohibited for elementary and middle school students. NC-17 and X-rated videos are not permitted.

GIFT MATERIALS
Donated materials must meet selection criteria; donors assume responsibility for valuation.

===== POLICY IJOA — Field Trips and Other Activities Away from School =====

OVERVIEW AND DEFINITIONS
The Poudre School District recognizes educational value in student participation in field trips and related activities. The policy defines covered activities as: enrichment and experiential educational activities (with or without course credit), extracurricular practices and competitions, and school-sponsored social, experiential, or fundraising activities.

RULES APPLICABLE TO ALL FIELD TRIPS AND ACTIVITIES — PERMISSION AND ELIGIBILITY
A District permission form must be signed by the parent or guardian of a student before the student may participate. Only current District students qualify, though recently graduated students may participate in activities they earned during enrollment.

STUDENTS WITH DISABILITIES
Determinations regarding the appropriateness of a field trip/activity, supervision requirements, necessary accommodations and/or modifications, and other matters concerning students with disabilities shall be made by the students' IEP or Section 504 team.

CHAPERONES AND SUPERVISION
Adult chaperones must be District employees or authorized volunteers, qualified and registered under District Policy KJ.

DISTRICT AUTHORITY
The District reserves the right to deny any request for a field trip/activity, to cancel any previously approved field trip/activity up to the time of departure, and to terminate or redirect any field trip/activity in progress for any reason deemed necessary or appropriate.

TRANSPORTATION
District vehicles, private vehicles, or commercial transportation may be used. Private vehicle drivers must hold valid licenses, acceptable insurance, and District approval.

===== POLICY IJOA-R — Field Trips =====

===== POLICY IKF-2025 — Graduation and Diploma Requirements =====

GRADUATION REQUIREMENTS
To obtain a high school diploma from Poudre School District, students entering 9th grade for the first time in 2025-2026 or later must satisfy academic standards encompassing: (1) College and Career Readiness Measures in Reading, Writing, and Communicating; Mathematics; and (2) PSD Core Credit Requirements.

COLLEGE AND CAREER READINESS MEASURES
Students must demonstrate competency by meeting or exceeding cut scores on one approved Reading, Writing, and Communicating measure and one Mathematics measure, or complete an alternative College and Career Readiness pathway. The district provides, at no cost to students, access to the English and Mathematics SAT assessment during the students' 11th grade year. Other assessment costs are borne by students unless waived per District Policy JQ and the Board-Adopted Student Fee Schedule.

Approved measures include: Accuplacer, ACT, ACT WorkKeys, Advanced Placement, ASVAB, SAT, Concurrent Enrollment Courses, District Capstone, Industry Certificates, and International Baccalaureate assessments. Cut scores follow Colorado State Board of Education standards with locally approved criteria detailed in administrative guidelines. Students may select any combination of measures to satisfy requirements.

All students must develop an Individual Career Academic Plan, which includes a review of graduation requirements, career exploration, and specific student course selection.

PSD CORE CREDIT REQUIREMENTS
Students must complete 240 total credits distributed as follows: Mathematics & Computer Science 30 credits (including algebra and geometry; Algebra II recommended for four-year college); Science 30 credits (physical, life, earth system sciences; Chemistry/Physics recommended); Language Arts 40 credits (oral expression, listening, reading, writing, research, reasoning); Social Studies 30 credits (geography, civics, history, economics; must include World History, U.S. History, Civics); Fine and Applied Arts 10 credits (original products, artifacts, performing, critical/creative problem-solving); Wellness 10 credits (physical and personal wellness); Financial Literacy 5 credits (personal financial literacy skills); Additional Coursework 85 credits.

PSD RECOMMENDED ADDITIONAL CREDITS
The district recommends but does not require completion of credits in: World Language (10-30), Health Curriculum (5), Geography (10), Humanities (5), and Economics (5). These shall not impede a student from earning their high school diploma if they are not completed.

GRADUATION COURSES TAUGHT IN MIDDLE SCHOOL
Students receive high school graduation credit for approved middle school courses upon earning a passing grade. District-approved curriculum or equivalent accredited coursework is required; partial credit shall not be awarded for completing one semester of a two-semester sequence in middle school. Parents may opt out of graduation credit designation through the student's 9th grade year, with grade record removal becoming permanent and non-reversible once declined.

ELIGIBILITY FOR LEARNERS WITH DIVERSE BACKGROUNDS — STUDENTS WITH DISABILITIES
The district ensures access to all graduation pathways and standard diploma opportunity regardless of alternative state assessment participation. A student's Individualized Education Plan (IEP) describes course sequencing, specially designed instruction, accommodations, and modifications enabling competency demonstration. Students on 504 Plans and IEPs access allowable accommodations on competency demonstrations.

MULTILINGUAL LEARNERS
Students identified for English Language Development (ELD) programs who arrived in the U.S. in 8th grade or later and were classified as Non-English proficient may follow an alternate pathway. ELD courses may contribute to fulfillment of English Language Arts, World Language, or other graduation requirements. Immigrant students lacking prior transcripts receive an equitable credit process recognizing prior learning.

WAIVERS OF GRADUATION REQUIREMENTS
Schools may request waivers for senior-year requirements per assistant superintendent guidelines. For students with disabilities, IEP teams may seek waivers if determined in the student's best educational interest or necessary to provide Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE). Waivers require approval by the assistant superintendent or designee and the student's IEP team, with documentation in permanent special education files.

ENDORSEMENT CATEGORIES
District endorsements align with Colorado Department of Education categories and requirements.

Adopted: April 9, 2024.

CROSS REFERENCES
IKC (Grade Point Averages/Class Ranking); IKFB (Graduation Exercises).

===== POLICY IMDC — Religious Observances and Displays =====

GENERAL PRINCIPLES
The district maintains religious neutrality and prohibits: organized worship services by district staff; religious indoctrination or prayers in school ceremonies; and advancement of any specific religion or religion over non-religion. Religious exhibits may be displayed only as an integral part of some curricular area such as art, music, history, or literature for informational purposes and with no intent to indoctrinate. Schools must consider major religious observances when scheduling picture day, open house, prom, graduation, tests, assignments, and field trips. When conflicts arise between a student's religious observances and the school's schedule of classes or activities, the religious observance takes precedence. Absences for religious observance shall not count against a student in determining exclusion, failure, or reduction of grades. Teachers must provide a meaningful opportunity and reasonable time to make up missed classwork, tests, quizzes, and final exam reviews.

===== POLICY IMDC-R — Teaching about Religion =====

INTRODUCTION
Poudre School District activities must not advance or inhibit religious belief or non-belief, nor discriminate between belief systems. Schools shall neither endorse nor interfere with religions. The district endorses the study of religion in an objective, respectful, and cross-cultural manner as integral to education.

NEUTRALITY
No religious belief or non-belief shall be promoted or disparaged by district or employees. The district encourages appreciation and respect for differing religious views. Students and staff may be excused from activities contrary to their religious beliefs when there are clear issues overriding concern that would prevent it, with alternative activities provided where appropriate.

TEACHING ABOUT RELIGION AND SCHOOL PROGRAMS
An education excluding religious references would be incomplete. Teaching must be about and not of religion in an open, objective, and respectful manner. The district supports inclusion of religious literature, music, drama, and arts when intrinsic to the learning experience and presented objectively. Religious themes may be included to enhance understanding that religion and diversity of beliefs are basic components of our cultural heritage, without fostering particular tenets or demeaning beliefs. School programs, performances, and celebrations must serve educational purposes. Religious content may be included if it has an independent education purpose which contributes to the stated objectives of approved curriculum, considering intent, effect, and timing.

STUDENT EXPRESSION OF RELIGIOUS BELIEFS
Students are free to express religious or non-religious beliefs in discussions, compositions, art, music, speech, and debate. No prayer, worship, or religious expression shall be prescribed or sanctioned, though this does not restrict spontaneous discussion about religion, personal prayer, or personal meditation that is non-disruptive and respects others' privacy. The district makes reasonable efforts to accommodate religious observances regarding required schoolwork and testing, with students responsible for advance notification and arranging make-ups. Students should be excused only to the extent that their ability to complete the educational program is not jeopardized.

PROCESS FOR DECISIONMAKING — PRINCIPLES FOR DECISIONMAKING
The Three-Part Test (from U.S. Supreme Court First Amendment jurisprudence) requires affirmative answers to: Purpose — is the activity educational? Effect — does it avoid promoting religion? Excessive Entanglement — does it avoid excessive relationships between schools and religious organizations? Decision makers must also determine consistency with policy intent respecting individual dignity and beliefs.

RESPONSIBILITY FOR DECISIONMAKING
Employees must ensure activities comply with policy. When specific religious content is included in lessons or activities, employees must seek counsel from immediate supervisors. Programs, performances, celebrations, classroom activities, visual displays, assemblies, sponsoring religious groups, and facility use for special programs require principal review before inclusion of religious content. Supervisors are responsible for verifying activities have been reviewed appropriately and meet the outlined tests.

TEACHING ABOUT RELIGION AND SCHOOL PROGRAMS — STANDARDS
Religious literature, music, drama, arts, and themes in social studies must comply with these regulations when included as significant elements achieving course objectives. Religious themes are defined as concepts which are identified with any religious traditions or tenet. Materials or activities must meet two criteria: positive answers to the three-part test, and determination that content is consistent with policy intent and curriculum objectives. Teachers must monitor material appearances and request principal review when questionable.

SCHOOL PROGRAMS AND DISPLAYS
To include religious music, symbols, art, or writings in programs, displays, performances, classroom activities, and celebrations, teachers must demonstrate an independent educational purpose consistent with the stated objectives of the approved curriculum. Prior review and regulation of content is necessary and appropriate given constitutional obligations. All relevant artistic, social, and cultural aspects should be considered. School programs means school-sponsored student performances or presentations to other classes, schools, parents, or the public. Religious content means music, objectives, symbols, art, readings, or writings that are identified with any religious tradition or which refer to any matter or religious concern.

PRIOR REVIEW PROCEDURES
Teachers may either confer with the principal to establish context and cooperative communication, or submit a written instructional plan including curricular objectives, specific religious content, educational purposes for that content, and program context. The principal applies the three-part test and exercises professional judgment on compliance and policy consistency. Special attention is given to participant and audience age/maturity, proximity to religious holidays, content diversity, overall program impression, and appropriate disclaimers. Within five school days, the principal responds in writing with approval or denial identifying noncompliance areas.

APPEAL PROCESS — FIRST LEVEL, INFORMAL DISCUSSION
The appealing party meets informally with immediate supervisor; decision made within two school days. The supervisor notifies the assistant superintendent of school services.

SECOND LEVEL
A written review request goes to the director of curriculum and instruction or designee. Both parties may present positions verbally or in writing. Decision made within five school days.

THIRD LEVEL
Either party may appeal to the assistant superintendent of school services or designee. A written request must be filed within five school days after the second-level decision, setting forth all disagreement reasons and including previous submissions. A written decision is provided within five school days. During the appeal process, the portion of the activity considered questionable shall not be practiced or performed.

No provisions regarding grade point averages, class ranking, graduation requirements, program placement, participation eligibility based on test scores, automated decision systems, algorithms, artificial intelligence, or machine learning appear in this policy.

===== POLICY IKC — Grade Point Averages/Class Rankings =====

APPLICABILITY
This policy applies to students graduating in 2028-2029 or later, and those enrolling in ninth grade during 2025-2026 or thereafter.

GRADE POINT AVERAGE CALCULATION
4.0 Scale Conversion: letter grades convert as follows: A = 4 points; B = 3 points; C = 2 points; D = 1 point; and F, WF (Withdraw Failing) and IE (Insufficient Evidence) = 0 points.

WEIGHTED GPA FOR ADVANCED COURSES
Advanced Placement, International Baccalaureate, and Concurrent/Dual Enrollment college courses receive enhanced point values: A=5, B=4, C=3, D=2, F=0 (compared to unweighted equivalents of 4, 3, 2, 1, 0).

COURSE WITHDRAWAL PROVISIONS
Students dropping courses after established deadlines receive automatic WF grades counted in GPA calculations, except with principal approval. Withdrawals before deadlines receive W grades excluded from GPA calculations.

DECILE-BASED CLASS RANKING
Class rank divides students into ten equal groups using weighted GPA. The top 10% occupy the first decile, the next 10% the second decile, and so forth, reflecting relative academic performance.

RECALCULATION REQUIREMENTS
Both weighted and unweighted GPAs and class rank are cumulative, recalculated after each grading term beginning in ninth grade.

Note: this policy establishes a formulaic, rules-based (non-AI) calculation method for GPA weighting and decile-based class ranking that directly determines students' academic standing; no automated decision system, algorithm (in the AI/ML sense), artificial intelligence, machine learning, or predictive tool is referenced — the "calculation" is a fixed arithmetic formula, not a learned/predictive model.
