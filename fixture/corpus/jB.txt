===== POLICY JICEC — Student Distribution of Non-School Materials =====

The policy grants students the right to distribute non-school materials on campus or at school events, subject to specific restrictions and procedures. "Non-school materials" refers to items not owned or sanctioned by the District that aren't used in curricular or extracurricular programs.

PROHIBITED CONTENT

Students cannot distribute materials that "create or threaten to create substantial disruption of District or school operations," disrupt the learning environment, cause injury to persons or property, are obscene, defamatory, or violate privacy rights, or encourage violation of laws or District policies.

APPROVAL PROCESS

Distributions exceeding 10 copies require principal notification at least three school days prior, with sample materials provided. The principal responds within three days with written approval or denial and explanation.

DISTRIBUTION PARAMETERS

Time: limited to 30 minutes before school, during lunch, or 15 minutes after school only. Place: designated locations only; never in classrooms or during activities. Manner: no coercion allowed; students must remove or recycle leftover materials.

APPEALS

Decisions may be appealed to the assistant superintendent within 10 days; the assistant superintendent's decision is final. This is the only human-review/appeal step identified in the policy.

DISCIPLINE

Violations of this policy shall be grounds for student discipline and denial of future distribution requests. No automated, algorithmic, AI, machine-learning, scoring, or predictive tools are mentioned anywhere in this policy.

===== POLICY JICF — Secret Societies/Gang Activity Dress =====

The Board prohibits student involvement in, promotion of, or membership indication in secret societies or gangs "on all District property, on all District vehicles, at all District or school-sponsored activities or events, and off District property when such conduct has a reasonable connection to the District."

KEY DEFINITIONS

Gang: "a group of three or more individuals with a common interest, bond or activity characterized by criminal or delinquent conduct." Delinquent Conduct: "conduct of a juvenile contrary to any federal or state law, any county or municipal ordinance except traffic offenses, or any lawful order of a court." Secret Society: "a group that possesses secret rites among its members and/or deals with private matters known only to one or a few and kept from others."

NON-DISCRIMINATION PROTECTION

District employees cannot infer gang or secret society membership "solely because of ethnic background, race, color, religion, socioeconomic status, or any other protected class."

ADMINISTRATIVE ACTIONS

School administrators must contact parents regarding possible involvement and may implement disciplinary measures as appropriate. The superintendent maintains communication with law enforcement and community agencies for information sharing.

No provisions regarding formal discipline procedures, hearings, appeals, automated decision systems, or algorithmic decision-making appear in this policy document.

===== POLICY JICH — Student Conduct Involving Drugs and Alcohol =====

The Poudre School District maintains a policy addressing student conduct related to drugs and alcohol through education, support, and enforcement mechanisms.

KEY DEFINITIONS

"Analogue": a substance producing stimulant, depressant, or hallucinogenic effects similar to controlled substances. "Drugs": substances defined as drugs or controlled substances under state/federal law, including analogues. "Authorized drugs": over-the-counter and prescription medications "properly possessed and used by the person for whom they are intended." "Unauthorized drugs": all drugs not classified as authorized. "Distributing": "a student's involvement in the exchange of drugs for anything of value."

PROHIBITED CONDUCT

The policy prohibits "possession of drug paraphernalia and possession, use, distribution, or being under the influence of alcohol, unauthorized drugs" on district property, vehicles, and at sponsored events.

DISCIPLINARY FRAMEWORK

First offense: five-day suspension with up to four days deferred upon completion of counseling or drug/alcohol programs. Second offense: ten-day suspension with up to five days deferred. Third offense: student considered for expulsion; may participate in intervention programs to avoid expulsion. Fourth and subsequent offenses: recommended for expulsion. Distribution: "a student will be considered for expulsion for distribution of any drugs on the first and any subsequent offenses."

The policy includes no mentions of automated systems, algorithms, AI, machine learning, scoring, or predictive tools.

===== POLICY JICI — Student Conduct Involving Weapons =====

The school board prohibits student possession, use, or threatened use of dangerous weapons on district property, vehicles, and school-sponsored events, as well as off-campus conduct with reasonable school connection.

KEY DEFINITIONS

Dangerous weapon includes: firearms (loaded or unloaded), projectile devices such as pellet guns, "any knife, regardless of blade length," and objects intended to inflict death or serious bodily injury. Firearm encompasses weapons designed to expel projectiles via explosives, frames/receivers, mufflers, silencers, and destructive devices.

MANDATORY CONSEQUENCES

Federal law requires "expulsion for no less than one full calendar year" for students bringing firearms to school. The superintendent may modify this requirement case-by-case in writing — this is a human-review/discretion step for the mandatory expulsion determination.

FIREARM FACSIMILES

Students cannot carry items resembling actual firearms without prior principal authorization. Violations may result in suspension or expulsion. The principal's decision on facsimile authorization is "final and not subject to appeal" — i.e., no appeal process exists for this determination.

VOLUNTARY SURRENDER OPTION

Students may surrender prohibited weapons (excluding firearms) to administrators without discipline if brought inadvertently without intent to use. Firearms always result in disciplinary consequences regardless of circumstances. Repeated surrenders may trigger discipline.

LAW ENFORCEMENT REFERRAL

District personnel shall refer any student to law enforcement who possesses firearms or dangerous weapons on campus or at school events. No automated, algorithmic, AI, or predictive determination process is mentioned; weapon determinations are made by administrators/principals.

===== POLICY JICJ — Student Possession and Use of Personal Communication Devices =====

The Board of Education acknowledges that personal technology devices (PTDs) can serve educational purposes and facilitate emergency communication, but restricts their use during the school day to "reduce distractions, support learning, and promote safety." Accommodations for disabilities are exempt from these limitations.

KEY DEFINITIONS

An "emergency situation" involves threats to life, injury, or property damage. "Personal technology device" encompasses cell phones, tablets, laptops, GPS devices, cameras, wearables, and similar portable electronics.

USAGE RESTRICTIONS

Students may use PTDs on school property only when use causes no "distraction or disruption to the learning environment"; during instruction, use must connect to curricula and receive explicit staff authorization; in classrooms/educational activities, use must be expressly authorized by administrators; on district vehicles, use must not interfere with safe operation. Emergency exceptions apply when PTD use doesn't impede emergency response. Recording in bathrooms or locker rooms is prohibited.

PERMITTED ACCOMMODATIONS

PTD use is allowed as a disability accommodation or English-language support when documented in a student's plan per Colorado and federal law.

CONSEQUENCES FOR VIOLATIONS

Violations may result in device confiscation (duration determined by principal, potentially through the school year), academic penalties (failing grades for cheating/dishonesty), discipline including suspension or expulsion, and law enforcement referral for legal violations.

Adoption dates: April 13, 2010 (effective July 1, 2010); Revised June 9, 2026 (effective July 1, 2026). No automated, algorithmic, AI, machine-learning, scoring, or predictive monitoring tools are referenced; device-use decisions rest with staff/administrators.

===== POLICY JIH — Searches =====

The policy establishes procedures for student searches to protect "the health, safety and welfare of students, staff, and members of the community."

KEY DEFINITIONS

Unauthorized: items dangerous to health/safety, disruptive to school functions, or described as unauthorized in school rules. Reasonable Suspicion: requires "specific and articulable facts and reasonable inferences" that a search will reveal policy violations; the standard demands both justification at inception and searches "reasonably related in scope to the circumstances."

SEARCH AUTHORITY CATEGORIES

Lockers/Desks: school property subject to "general inspections" without notice, consent, or warrant. Vehicles: interiors and trunks may be searched upon reasonable suspicion of illegal items. Personal Searches: permitted when reasonable suspicion exists; safety checks conducted as part of documented safety plans require a "private" setting with "at least one other District employee" present. Canine Inspections: trained dogs may sniff lockers, vehicles, and students; an alert "can constitute reasonable suspicion for a search." Metal Detectors: authorized when reasonable suspicion exists, following a pattern of weapons, or after violence incidents.

PROCEDURAL PROTECTIONS

More intrusive searches beyond removing outer clothing require "law enforcement officer" involvement. Weapon possession triggers mandatory law enforcement contact.

EVIDENCE HANDLING

Seized items are tagged, secured, and held until "no longer needed" for disciplinary matters, after which parents may retrieve items or administrators dispose of them. All search-decision authority in this policy rests with human staff/administrators/law enforcement; no automated, algorithmic, AI, or predictive determination system is mentioned.

===== POLICY JJ — Interscholastic Athletic Training and Personal Conduct Rules =====

This policy establishes conduct standards for student athletes in grades 9-12 participating in district interscholastic sports programs. The document emphasizes that athletic participation is "a privilege, not a right" and requires year-round compliance.

GENERAL REQUIREMENTS

Student athletes must comply with district Code of Conduct policies, Colorado High School Activities Association bylaws, and individual team rules. They remain ineligible during any suspension or expulsion period.

SUBSTANCE AND TOBACCO PROHIBITIONS

The policy prohibits "use or possess[ion of] any beverage containing alcohol," tobacco products, or controlled substances on a year-round basis, including off-campus and non-school activities.

VIOLATION CONSEQUENCES

First violation: 30% competition suspension. Second violation: 50% competition suspension. Third violation: permanent athletic ineligibility through graduation. During suspensions, athletes must maintain practice participation and "remain in good standing" with their teams.

This policy contains no mention of automated decision systems, algorithms, AI tools, machine learning, predictive scoring, technology monitoring, formal hearing procedures, or appeals processes for violations.

===== POLICY JJG — Contests for Students =====

This policy establishes procedures for reviewing student contest participation requests. A panel including the assistant superintendent of school services evaluates proposals.

KEY APPROVAL CRITERIA

Participation in the contest by students or classes must be relevant to the major objectives of the district; appropriate grade level alignment with instructional programs; deadlines that don't disrupt normal instruction; noncommercial nature of the activity; student motivation enhancement; positive public relations value; self-image building for participants; compliance with Colorado High School Activities Association guidelines for senior high events.

RECOMMENDED OUTCOMES

Three options: full-scale participation across appropriate classes, voluntary teacher/student participation, or no participation.

Adopted February 1975; revised December 1982, May 1988, and October 28, 1996. This document contains no references to discipline procedures, hearings, appeals, student data privacy, technology monitoring, automated systems, algorithms, artificial intelligence, or predictive tools.

===== POLICY JK — Student Discipline =====

Policy JK addresses student discipline and rights of students with disabilities, along with procedures for disciplining students with disabilities.

OBJECTIVE AND APPROACH

The policy establishes that discipline aims to maintain safe schools and help students develop self-control. The district emphasizes "restorative practices" and positive learning environments, considering prevention and intervention approaches where feasible.

DEFINITIONS

Behavioral intervention plan (BIP): a plan for students with IEPs addressing inappropriate behaviors. Restorative practices: methods emphasizing repair of harm caused by misconduct. Serious bodily injury: involves substantial risk of death, extreme pain, disfigurement, or functional loss.

EXPULSION PREVENTION

Expulsion should be a last resort. Districts must consider alternative remedies and provide support services to at-risk students through partnerships with community organizations.

DISCIPLINARY INFORMATION SHARING

Principals must communicate discipline details to teachers and counselors having direct contact with the student, while maintaining confidentiality under FERPA guidelines — this is the policy's central provision governing use of student (discipline) data.

STUDENTS WITH DISABILITIES — KEY PROVISIONS

Students with disabilities may be suspended up to 10 school days annually. Beyond 10 days constitutes a "disciplinary change of placement" requiring educational services. A "manifestation determination" must occur within 10 school days to assess whether behavior relates to the disability — this is a required human-review step performed by a team (not an automated process).

SPECIAL CIRCUMSTANCES FOR ALTERNATIVE PLACEMENT

The district may place students with disabilities in alternative settings for up to 45 days without manifestation review if the student carried weapons, possessed illegal drugs, sold controlled substances, or inflicted serious bodily injury.

SUSPECTED DISABILITIES

Students suspected of having disabilities receive protections if the district has "knowledge" through written parent concerns or teacher observations. No automated, algorithmic, AI, machine-learning, scoring, or predictive tools are referenced anywhere in this policy; all determinations (manifestation determination, placement, discipline) are made by human staff/teams.

===== POLICY JKA — Physical Intervention, Restraint, Seclusion and Time-Out =====

This district policy establishes procedures for physical intervention, restraint, seclusion, and time-out with students to maintain safe learning environments. Actions complying with this policy are not considered child abuse or corporal punishment.

PHYSICAL INTERVENTION

District employees may physically intervene without constituting "restraint" when "reasonably necessary to protect persons or property, prevent an act of wrongdoing and/or maintain student discipline."

KEY DEFINITIONS

Restraint: methods limiting freedom of movement, excluding physician-recommended devices, protective equipment, adaptive devices, and medical positioning devices. Seclusion: isolated placement preventing egress. Time-out: non-exclusionary removal of reinforcers, or exclusionary separation at three levels (contingent observation, exclusion, isolation).

RESTRAINT GUIDELINES

Employees may use physical restraint in non-face-down positions when students pose a "serious, probable and imminent threat of bodily injury." Face-down restraints and mechanical/chemical restraints are prohibited except for students displaying deadly weapons. Restraint must not impede breathing or communication; must be administered only by trained employees; must be released within fifteen minutes except for safety reasons; and requires continuous monitoring.

SECLUSION GUIDELINES

Permissible only when students pose a "serious, probable and imminent threat of bodily injury." Rooms must be adequately lit, minimum 6x6 feet, with constant observation by qualified staff immediately outside.

TRAINING, NOTIFICATION, AND REVIEW

Staff using restraint require training including de-escalation and physical management techniques, with retraining every two years. Parents/guardians must be notified in writing before potential use. Written incident reports must be submitted within one school day. Comprehensive reviews are documented annually by the superintendent. All decisions to restrain/seclude are made by trained human staff in the moment; no automated, algorithmic, AI, or predictive tool is mentioned.

===== POLICY JKB — Detention of Students =====

This district policy permits school administrators to detain students after regular hours as a disciplinary alternative to suspension or exclusion.

GROUNDS FOR DETENTION

Detention may be imposed for "violation of the code of conduct and behavior that is disruptive," per District Policy JK regarding student discipline.

===== POLICY JKBA — Disciplinary Removal from Classroom =====

This district policy governs how teachers may remove students from classrooms for disruptive conduct. Removals constitute in-school suspensions and typically last one to three days.

KEY DEFINITIONS

Disciplinary removal: "removal of a student from the teacher's classroom to another location at the school after the day on which the disruptive behavior occurs." Willful behavior: conscious, purposeful actions where students know or disregard whether disruption will result. Material and substantial disruption: actions causing "serious interference with the orderly operation of the school."

REQUIRED PROCEDURES

Before imposing removal, teachers must consult administrators, who make the final determination on appropriateness — establishing a mandatory human-review step by an administrator before removal is finalized. Students and families must receive notice of reasons and duration.

BEHAVIOR PLAN REQUIREMENTS

First removal: develop a behavior plan with student and family input. Second removal: review and revise the plan; assess whether the school failed to implement it. Third and subsequent removals (secondary level): removal may extend through the grading term, only after written disclosure in the prior behavior plan.

STUDENT PROTECTIONS

Students receive coursework during removal periods. Those removed through the grading term may "complete and receive equivalent credit for the course." Special education students are protected under separate policies addressing IEP and Section 504 plan requirements.

Originally adopted August 14, 2000; most recently revised June 9, 2026 (effective July 1, 2026). No automated, algorithmic, AI, machine-learning, scoring, or predictive tools are referenced; classroom-removal decisions are made by teachers and administrators.

===== POLICY JKC — Discipline of Habitually Disruptive Students =====

The policy defines a "habitually disruptive student" as one who has caused "a material and substantial disruption on school grounds, at a District-sanctioned activity or event or in a District vehicle three or more times" within a school year. "Material and substantial disruption" refers to "behavior that causes a serious interference with the orderly operation of the school" or its educational capacity, including actions harmful to others' welfare or safety.

NOTIFICATION REQUIREMENTS

Students and parents receive written notice of each disruption counted toward the habitually disruptive designation, plus written and telephone notification of the definition and potential consequences.

BEHAVIOR PLANNING

Following the first or second counted disruption, school administrators may develop a behavior plan with teacher and staff input, encouraging parental participation. Plans should address the disruptive conduct and include goals, objectives, and timelines for behavioral modification.

PLAN DOCUMENTATION

The behavior plan informs the student of consequences including "discipline, including suspension or expulsion." Copies go to the student, parent, responsible staff member, and the student's file.

ONGOING MANAGEMENT

Additional disruptions are handled per the developed behavior plan if one exists.

CONSEQUENCES

Once declared habitually disruptive, the principal may "authorize a suspension or initiate expulsion proceedings" after reviewing the student's records and prior incidents — a human, records-based review performed by the principal. This policy contains no references to automated decision systems, algorithms, AI tools, or predictive scoring mechanisms; the habitually-disruptive determination and consequence decisions are made by school administrators based on documented incident counts, not by any automated system.

===== POLICY JKD-JKE — Suspension/Expulsion of Students =====

This policy establishes procedures for suspending or expelling students while ensuring parental involvement and due process protections. The board considers six factors when making disciplinary decisions: the student's age; the student's disciplinary history; the student's eligibility as a student with a disability; and three others including violation severity, safety threats, and whether lesser interventions would suffice.

DISCIPLINARY ALTERNATIVES

Prior to suspension or expulsion, principals may employ alternatives including detention, in-school suspension, counseling, mental health support, restorative justice programs, behavioral plans, and "Participation in the District's restorative justice/restorative practices program."

SUSPENSION AUTHORITY

Principals can suspend third-grade and higher students for up to five school days under certain grounds, or ten days under others. For preschool through second grade, suspensions are limited to three days unless "a longer period of suspension is necessary to resolve the safety threat."

EXPULSION PROCEDURES

The superintendent holds authority to expel students for periods not exceeding one year. The policy requires that "the superintendent, in accordance with the limitations imposed by C.R.S. § 22-33-101 through -110" make expulsion determinations — expulsion is a human determination made by the superintendent. Students or parents may appeal expulsion decisions to the board in writing, providing a human appeal/review pathway.

POST-EXPULSION SERVICES

Within five days of expulsion, districts must notify families of educational services available to expelled students designed to "provide a second chance for the student to succeed."

This policy contains no mentions of AI, algorithms, predictive tools, automated decision systems, or technology monitoring in disciplinary processes.

===== POLICY JKD-JKE-R — Procedures Regarding Suspension/Expulsion of Students =====

This school district policy establishes procedures for student discipline through suspension and expulsion.

SUSPENSION PROCEDURES

The policy requires "notice of the contemplated action" including "a statement of what the student is accused of doing" and "the basis for the allegation" before removal occurs, except in emergencies involving "danger to people or property or ongoing threat of disrupting the academic process." Students receive a due process meeting where they "explain their alleged conduct and surrounding circumstances" with opportunity to call witnesses. Following suspension decisions, parents receive written notification and the student may make up schoolwork "during the period of suspension for full credit."

EXPULSION PROCEDURES

The district must provide written notice and allow hearing requests within specified timeframes. The policy states the district bears "the burden to prove, based on a preponderance of the evidence, that the student's actions constitute grounds for expulsion." Expulsion hearings include several protections: a trained hearing officer without conflicts of interest, student representation by counsel or advocate, witness testimony opportunities, and audio recording of proceedings. "Formal rules of evidence will not apply" but testimony occurs "under oath." These provisions establish a full human hearing/appeal process (hearing officer, testimony, evidentiary burden) governing suspension and expulsion decisions.

SPECIAL RESTRICTIONS

Expelled students face enrollment prohibitions in schools where crime victims or their family members work or study, with specific limitations outlined in the policy.

The document contains no references to automated decision systems, algorithms, AI, machine learning, or predictive tools.

===== POLICY JKDA-JKEA — Grounds for Suspension/Expulsion of Students =====

FOR THIRD GRADE AND HIGHER

Students face discipline for conduct including: property destruction, assault, weapons/drugs/alcohol violations, defiance of authority, behavior threatening safety, disruption of operations, threats of harm, repeated interference with education, false accusations, and being declared habitually disruptive. A "habitually disruptive student" is defined as one who has caused "a material and substantial disruption three or more times" during a school year on school grounds or at school events.

FOR PRESCHOOL THROUGH SECOND GRADE

Suspension/expulsion is limited to: weapons policy violations, drugs/alcohol policy violations, and "conduct that endangers the health or safety of others." The policy notes such action requires a determination that removal "would create a safety threat that cannot otherwise be addressed" — a human safety-threat determination required before removal of the youngest students.

ADDITIONAL GROUNDS

Under Colorado law, expulsion may be based on physical/mental disability preventing educational benefit or causing risk to other students' welfare.
