===== POLICY JLCB — IMMUNIZATION OF STUDENTS =====

The district's health services coordinator must provide parents/guardians with Colorado's standardized immunization document annually, including required and recommended vaccines, age guidelines, and the school's vaccination rates compared to state standards. Parents/guardians must also receive information about their right to seek an exemption from immunization requirements.

ATTENDANCE WITHOUT STANDARD IMMUNIZATION

Students may attend district schools without standard immunizations only if they present one of four alternatives:

1. Parental Authorization: a signed request for local health officials to administer immunizations.

2. Medical Exemption: a completed certificate of medical exemption from a licensed physician, qualified physician assistant, or advanced practice nurse documenting that immunization poses health risks, submitted once and kept on file.

3. Online Education Module: a certificate showing completion of the Colorado Department of Public Health and Environment module, signed by parent/guardian or eligible student, submitted annually.

4. Nonmedical Exemption: a certificate signed annually by both parent/guardian (or eligible student) and a healthcare provider, maintained on file each year.

COMMUNICABLE DISEASE PROVISION

During outbreaks of vaccine-preventable diseases, exempted students may be excluded from attendance.

===== POLICY JLCD — ADMINISTERING MEDICINE TO STUDENTS/ASTHMA, ALLERGY AND ANAPHYLAXIS =====

This policy governs medicine administration to students, excluding medical marijuana (covered under Policy JLCDB). Violations of possession, use, or distribution contrary to this policy constitute violations of Policy JICH regarding drugs and alcohol.

KEY DEFINITIONS

Emergency-use epinephrine is a portable, disposable drug delivery device approved by the Federal Food and Drug Administration that contains a premeasured, single dose. Medicine includes both prescription and nonprescription items. Nonprescription medicine encompasses over-the-counter products, homeopathic and herbal medicines, vitamins, and supplements. Opioid antagonist means naloxone or a similar FDA-approved non-controlled substance used to treat overdose. An Opioid-related Drug Overdose Event is an acute condition involving decreased consciousness or respiratory depression requiring medical assistance.

GENERAL ADMINISTRATION REQUIREMENTS

Students should take medicine outside school when possible. When necessary at school or school-sponsored activities, medicine may be administered by parents/caregivers or authorized District employees.

Written requests and liability releases signed by parent/caregiver and health care provider (MD, NP, PA, or DO) are mandatory. Separate documentation is required for each medicine and dosage change.

Verbal Request Exception: the school nurse may honor verbal requests only when prior written submission was impossible, the nurse confirms the caller's identity, and confirmation occurs with the prescribing provider. Written documentation must follow before second-day administration.

PRESCRIPTION MEDICINE RULES

Prescription medicine must arrive in original pharmacy-labeled containers displaying student name, medicine name, dosage, prescriber name, fill date, and expiration date.

Administration Authority: school nurses, nurse's designees (with principal approval and proper training), authorized high school students, and students authorized for asthma/allergy/anaphylaxis self-administration may administer prescription medicine. Administration requires written authorization and directions from the prescribing health care provider.

NONPRESCRIPTION MEDICINE RULES

Nonprescription medicine must arrive in original commercially-labeled containers.

Administration Authority: school nurses, nurse's designees (principal-approved), authorized high school students, and authorized asthma/allergy/anaphylaxis students may administer nonprescription medicine. Written authorization and directions from a treating health care provider are required for employee administration.

STUDENT SELF-ADMINISTRATION AUTHORIZATION

High school students and certain middle school students on international trips may possess and self-administer medicine (excluding medical marijuana and controlled substances) when: (1) a written parent/caregiver request and liability release are submitted; (2) required health care provider authorization is provided; (3) the school nurse and principal determine the student demonstrates proper administration ability, maturity, and responsibility; (4) the school nurse and principal determine possession poses no significant harm risk; (5) the student maintains only a one-day dose (unless the school nurse authorizes more for extended activities; insulin pumps are exempt from the one-day limit); (6) the student maintains medicine security; and (7) self-administration remains a revocable privilege.

ASTHMA, FOOD ALLERGY, AND ANAPHYLAXIS MANAGEMENT

Self-Carry Authorization Requirements: students with asthma, food allergies, or related life-threatening conditions may self-carry prescribed medication at school, school-sponsored activities, and in school vehicles when the following prerequisites are met: written medical authorization from the prescribing health care practitioner with medication name, purpose, dosage, frequency, and administration intervals; practitioner confirmation that the student received instruction and demonstrates capability; student demonstration of skill including identifying correct medication, demonstrating correct technique, understanding dosage and frequency, and recognizing administration timing; a written treatment plan developed by the school nurse collaborating with the health care practitioner (effective for the current school year or until a new plan is created); and a written contract signed by the school nurse, student, and parent/caregiver assigning responsibility levels.

Student Contract Requirements: the student will demonstrate medication competency and asthma/allergy management skills, notify officials when emergency medication is used or condition worsens, and not allow others to use the medication.

Parent/Caregiver Contract Requirements: the parent/caregiver will provide written medication orders and written authorization for possession and self-administration, ensure proper pharmacy/practitioner labeling, verify the device contains medication and hasn't expired, provide backup medication for emergencies, and regularly review allergy status with the student.

School Nurse Contract Requirements: the nurse will review correct technique with the student, be informed of dosage/timing orders and appropriate medication use, ensure regular status reviews, notify relevant employees on a need-to-know basis, and assign employee(s) to make 911 emergency calls if exposure requires epinephrine.

Liability Release: the parent/caregiver must sign a statement releasing the District, school, associated entities, employees, and volunteers from liability except for willful and wanton conduct or treatment plan disregard.

Emergency Epinephrine Administration Protocol: after emergency-use epinephrine administration at school, school-sponsored activities, or school vehicles, the student must immediately proceed to the school nurse. The school must promptly call 911, and authorized employees must provide appropriate follow-up care.

Food Allergy and Anaphylaxis Management: districts must make available on websites and at schools the Colorado Department of Public Health and Environment standard form allowing parents/caregivers to provide food allergy information. Schools must have plans for communication with emergency medical services, including sharing student food allergy forms with emergency responders. Parents/caregivers must supply prescribed medication for students not authorized to self-carry; emergency medications must be stored in secure, accessible locations for designated employees.

Employee Training: school nurses and principals must identify employees (particularly those working with allergic students) for emergency anaphylaxis treatment training covering food allergy understanding and reasonable avoidance measures, anaphylaxis symptom recognition, appropriate response procedures, and self-injectable epinephrine administration.

Health Plan Development: school nurses must develop Health Plans for each student with potential life-threatening food allergies, coordinating with IEP and Section 504 Plan teams if applicable. Plans must consider information from the student, parent/caregiver, and health care provider (including Food Allergy Forms) and include reasonable exposure-reduction steps.

Administrative Guidelines: health services coordinators must develop, review, and revise administrative guidelines for proper food allergy and anaphylaxis management. District employees working with allergic students must comply with these guidelines.

STOCK EMERGENCY-USE EPINEPHRINE

If supplies and funding permit, the District maintains a stock epinephrine supply for emergency anaphylaxis events on school grounds (excluding buses). Administration must comply with state law and State Board of Education rules. This stock supply does not replace student-specific medications provided by parents/caregivers.

OPIOID ANTAGONIST PROVISIONS

When funding and supplies are available, the District may maintain opioid antagonist stock supplies on school grounds, school buses, or both. District employees may administer opioid antagonists to students or others per state law, provided they receive appropriate training including overdose risk factors, overdose recognition, emergency services procedures, rescue breathing, and antagonist administration, developed by the health services coordinator. District employees may furnish opioid antagonists to any individual on school grounds or school buses. Employees administering opioid antagonists per policy and law are not subject to civil liability or criminal prosecution. School principal's designees may provide student training before furnishing opioid antagonists, and student possession or administration is not a policy violation.

SYNTHETIC OPIATE DETECTION TESTS

If funding and supplies are available, the District may maintain supplies of non-laboratory synthetic opiate detection tests and non-laboratory additive detection tests. District employees may furnish these tests to any individual per Student Services Department guidelines. Students may lawfully possess non-laboratory synthetic opiate detection tests or non-laboratory additive detection tests on school grounds, school buses, or at school-sponsored events without policy violation.

===== POLICY JLCDB — ADMINISTERING MEDICAL MARIJUANA TO QUALIFIED STUDENTS =====

This district policy establishes procedures for administering medical marijuana to qualified students, complying with Colorado law while protecting federal funding eligibility.

KEY DEFINITIONS

Designated location means a location identified in writing by the District in its sole discretion, including school grounds, buses, or sponsored events. Medical marijuana means cannabis products with THC concentration exceeding 0.3 percent used for addressing debilitating medical conditions. Permissible forms are non-smokeable products such as oils, tinctures, edible products or lotions administered and absorbed quickly; patches may be approved case-by-case. Primary caregiver means a parent, guardian, or licensed healthcare provider employed by the family, subject to visitor policies. Qualified student means one holding a valid physician recommendation and state registration, unable to receive treatment outside school hours. Volunteer means a District employee voluntarily administering marijuana per a written plan.

ADMINISTRATION REQUIREMENTS

Medical marijuana administration requires: (1) parent-provided physician recommendation and Colorado state registration documentation maintained in confidential medical records; (2) parent assumption of responsibility and liability release; (3) primary caregiver provision of permissible forms; (4) a District determination that location and method present no educational disruption or student exposure risk; (5) District storage options in locked containers with emergency accessibility, or immediate caregiver removal; (6) parental retrieval coordination, with District disposal if unclaimed reasonably; and (7) a written plan developed jointly identifying form, location, physician instructions, and protocols, signed by administrator, student (if capable), and parent.

RESTRICTIONS AND LIMITATIONS

This policy grants no right to demand access to specific locations. Federal property restrictions apply. Student possession or self-administration is strictly prohibited. The policy suspends immediately if federal funding loss is documented. Non-compliance results in revocation of administration privileges.

VOLUNTARY EMPLOYEE PARTICIPATION

Employees cannot be required, pressured, or directed to volunteer. School nurses retain discretion regarding involvement. Absence of a designated volunteer does not obligate District provision of alternatives.

ADOPTION/REVISION DATES

Adopted: May 24, 2022; Revised: June 9, 2026.


===== POLICY JLCDC — MEDICALLY NECESSARY TREATMENT IN SCHOOL SETTING =====

This policy governs how districts must handle medically necessary treatment for students provided by private health-care specialists, ensuring compliance with federal disability law while maintaining district authority over school operations.

DEFINITIONS

Medically necessary treatment is care that a Colorado licensed health-care provider acting within the scope of the health-care provider's license recommends or orders. The medically necessary team comprises district staff, the student's parent/caregiver, parent-invited individuals, the school principal/administrator, general education teacher, assistant director of Integrated Services or Student Services, and other appropriate staff including IEP or Section 504 team members. A private health-care specialist is any person licensed, certified, or otherwise authorized to provide health-care services in Colorado, including pediatric behavioral health and autism services providers under specified statutes; district staff cannot qualify as private specialists under this policy.

NOTICE TO PARENTS/CAREGIVERS

Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act provide students access to medically necessary treatment essential for meaningful educational access or safe school attendance. Treatment not meeting these standards falls outside this policy's scope.

DETERMINATION PROCESS

Timeline and Initiation: parents submit requests using the district's form. The district schedules meetings as soon as reasonably practicable, and no later than 30 calendar days.

Team Responsibility: the medically necessary team determines whether treatment must occur in school settings to ensure educational access under Section 504 and ADA Title II.

Provider Participation: teams must invite the treating health-care specialist not less than ten calendar days in advance, with options for written submissions.

District Authority: the policy clarifies that districts may use qualified internal staff or contract providers rather than accepting third-party specialists, and nothing requires permitting third parties to determine special education services in ways conflicting with federal law obligations.

IEP/504 Coordination: following determination, the relevant team meets to discuss FAPE provision if necessary.

ACCESS TO SCHOOL PREMISES

Direct Treatment Access: private specialists may provide medically necessary treatment per team determination, subject to Policy KI (Visitors to Schools) and regulation JLCDC-R.

Observation and Collaboration: specialists may access school property to observe the student in the school setting or collaborate with school personnel, similarly subject to applicable policies. District staff may observe simultaneously.

Limitations: permission may be limited or revoked if the private health-care specialist violates this policy or District Regulation JLCDC-R, or demonstrates inability to follow requirements responsibly.

APPEAL PROCEDURE

When teams deny medically necessary treatment in schools, parents receive notice of appeal rights. Appeals must comply with these requirements: written notice to the assistant superintendent within ten school days; the District holds a hearing within ten school days of the appeal request; advance notice of hearing date, time, and location is provided; the hearing is conducted by a neutral individual without direct outcome interest; full opportunity to present relevant evidence is afforded; the right to representation at parent/student expense, including an attorney (with five school-day advance notice), applies; a written decision is issued within a reasonable timeframe; the decision is based solely on hearing evidence, including a summary and reasoning; and if the determination is overturned, the matter is remanded to the appropriate team with recommendations.

REPORTING REQUIREMENTS

Each school designates staff to regularly report to the superintendent: requesting student names, requests, and outcomes (accepted/denied).

LEGAL REFERENCES

42 U.S.C. §§ 1396, 1396d(r)(5) (Medicaid coverage requirement); C.R.S. 22-20-121 (medically necessary treatment in school settings); C.R.S. 25.5, articles 4, 5, 6 (state medical assistance); C.R.S. 10-16-104(1.4) (autism services); Section 504, Rehabilitation Act of 1973, 29 U.S.C. § 794; Title II, Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990.

CROSS REFERENCES

JLCD – Administering Medications to Students; JLCDB – Administration of Medical Marijuana to Qualified Students; JLCDC-R – Authorizing Private Health-Care Specialists; KI – Visitors in Schools.

===== POLICY JLCDC-R — AUTHORIZING PRIVATE HEALTH-CARE SPECIALISTS FOR TREATMENT =====

This regulation implements Policy JLCDC and operates under section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, 29 U.S.C. sec. 794, and Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990.

INITIAL REQUIREMENTS BEFORE TREATMENT BEGINS

Written Treatment Plan: the district must prepare a plan identifying treatment form, location(s), and administration protocol with input from the specialist and parent/guardian. It must be signed by the administrator, student (if capable), specialist, and parent/guardian.

District Representative: the district must designate a representative with authority to schedule and cancel specialist visits.

Parental Consent: the student's parent signs a parental consent form to any medically necessary treatment in the school setting.

Confidentiality Affidavit: the specialist must commit to complying with the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA) and the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA).

Insurance Certificate: proof of General Liability, Auto Liability, and Professional Liability insurance meeting district standards is required.

Colorado Licensure: the private health-care specialist provides proof of Colorado licensure.

Assumption of Risk: the specialist waives claims for harm caused by the student or other students.

CONDITIONS DURING TREATMENT

Parties must avoid disruption to the learning environment and maintain instructional program integrity. The specialist must provide at least two weeks' advance notice for additional visits. The district retains discretion to deny, reschedule, or modify visits with two-week notice (except emergencies). The student's parent/guardian will be solely responsible for compensating the private health-care specialist. The specialist must follow all applicable state, federal, and district laws and policies. The district won't exercise supervisory control but may review services for coordination. Permission may be revoked if the specialist violates policy or fails to follow parameters.

===== POLICY JLCD-R — ADMINISTERING MEDICINE TO STUDENTS =====

These regulations exclude medical marijuana, which is governed separately under Policy JLCDB.

STORAGE OF MEDICINE

Medicines must be stored in the office or similar area at each school in a clean, locked cabinet, drawer, or other appropriate container restricted to medicine only and inaccessible to students. For refrigerated medicines, storage must be either (a) in a locked refrigerator reserved and used exclusively for the storage of medicine, or (b) in an impervious secondary container in a food storage refrigerator, separated from food and inaccessible to students.

At year's end, all stored medicine must be returned to the parent or guardian who furnished it or shall be appropriately disposed of by the school nurse.

HEALTH CARE PROVIDER AUTHORIZATION AND DIRECTIONS

Provider authorization must be written and signed, specifying: (a) the student's name; (b) name of the medicine; (c) purpose of the medicine; (d) dosage; (e) time(s) the medicine is to be administered; (f) manner of administration; (g) anticipated number of days the medicine needs to be administered at school and/or school-sponsored activities; and (h) possible side-effects.

SAFETY PRECAUTIONS

Before administration, personnel must verify: (a) a current request to administer medicine and release signed by the student's parent/guardian is on file; (b) written authorization and directions signed by the student's health care provider is on file; (c) the correct student is receiving the medicine; (d) the correct medicine is being administered; (e) the correct dose of medicine is being administered; (f) the medicine is being administered at the correct time; (g) the medicine has not expired; and (h) the medicine is being administered in the correct manner.

MEDICATION ERRORS

Errors include giving medicine to the wrong student, giving the wrong medicine to a student, giving the wrong dose, and related failures. Errors in administering medicine to a student must be promptly reported to the school nurse and to the student's parent or guardian, and must be documented on a Medication Error Report form. Critical errors require immediate contact with poison control or 911.

RECORDKEEPING

Individual records must include: (a) the name of the medicine; (b) the date and time the medicine was administered; (c) the dosage administered; (d) the manner the medicine was administered; (e) any unusual reactions or responses of the student; and (f) the name of the person administering the medicine. Records are maintained until the year of the student's twenty-first birthday.

===== POLICY JLDAC — SCREENING/TESTING OF STUDENTS =====

This district policy establishes parental and student rights regarding surveys, assessments, and evaluations. It grants parents/caregivers and eligible students (age 18+ or emancipated minors) the right to review such materials, whether created internally or by third parties.

KEY PROTECTIONS REQUIRING CONSENT

Students cannot be required to participate in assessments revealing information about: political affiliations or beliefs of the student or student's family; mental/psychological conditions; sexual behavior and attitudes; illegal or self-incriminating behavior; critical appraisals of family members; privileged relationships (lawyers, clergy, physicians); income (except for program eligibility determination); religious practices or beliefs; and social security numbers.

The policy also prohibits collection and marketing of personal student information without prior written consent.

NOTICE REQUIREMENTS

Schools must provide written notice at least two weeks before administering such assessments, making documents available for review and offering information about records examined, examination methods, purposes, and access procedures.

EXCEPTIONS

Exceptions include journalism student work, mandatory child abuse reporting, health professional evaluations, suicide/threat assessments, and educational product/service recruitment activities.

PHYSICAL SCREENINGS

Vision and hearing tests occur in specified grades. Parents may object on religious or personal grounds. The district must provide advance notice for non-emergency physical examinations.

BEHAVIOR TESTING

Staff cannot recommend psychotropic drugs or conduct behavior testing without parental written notice and permission.

ADOPTION/REVISIONS

Originally adopted April 1972; most recently revised June 9, 2026 (effective July 1, 2026).


===== POLICY JLIB — STUDENT DISMISSAL PRECAUTIONS =====

A child may receive an exemption from school attendance in accordance with applicable law. Schools bear legal responsibility for student health and safety throughout the instructional day. Therefore, each school must establish procedures to authenticate early dismissal requests, ensuring students depart only for legitimate reasons and to appropriate individuals.

TEACHER AND PRINCIPAL AUTHORITY

Teachers cannot release any student before the school day concludes or to anyone's care without prior written approval from the building principal.

The principal cannot excuse students under 18 years old before day's end without a parental request for early dismissal. Following this requirement, these practices apply: (1) no student shall be released from school early on the basis of an invalidated telephone call; and (2) children of estranged parents shall be released only upon the request of the parent whom a Colorado court holds directly responsible for the child and who is the parent or guardian registered on the school record.

ELEMENTARY STUDENTS

Elementary students must depart only with their parent or, if unavailable, with an authorized adult representative.

Principals or designated representatives shall implement supplementary protective measures as circumstances warrant.

ADOPTION AND REVISION HISTORY

Current practice codified 1995. Adopted: May 22, 1995. Revised: January 22, 1996.


===== POLICY JLIE — STUDENT VEHICLE USE AND PARKING =====

Students may park vehicles only in designated student lots, with vehicles remaining parked during class times unless the student is excused from school. Students must not congregate or loiter in parked cars or parking lots during the school day.

PRIVILEGE AND REVOCATION

Parking constitutes a revocable privilege subject to removal for irresponsible or dangerous vehicle use. This includes speeding, reckless maneuvers, or transporting passengers on the vehicle's exterior. Such conduct may result in suspension or expulsion.

SEARCH AUTHORIZATION

By bringing a vehicle to campus, students consent to searches when reasonable suspicion the search will yield evidence of illegal or unauthorized materials exists. Refusal by a student, parent/caregiver, or vehicle owner to allow a search results in privilege revocation without hearing or appeal rights and may constitute grounds for suspension or expulsion.

SCHOOL AUTHORITY

Schools may patrol student parking lots, visually inspect student motor vehicles, and use dogs trained to alert at the scent of drugs, explosives and other illegal or unauthorized materials. Principals retain authority to establish additional regulations as needed.

ADOPTION/REVISION HISTORY

Adopted February 1974; most recently revised by Board January 27, 2026, effective July 1, 2026.

===== POLICY JQ — STUDENT FEES, FINES AND CHARGES =====

The district prohibits charging fees as an enrollment or attendance condition for academic coursework, though textbook rentals, consumable materials, and miscellaneous fees are permitted per state law.

The policy allows the district to require payment for textbook fees, fees for expendable materials, fees for items students retain or consume, and other miscellaneous fees, along with voluntary enrichment activities and parking privileges.

LOSS/DAMAGE PROVISIONS

===== POLICY JRA-JRC — STUDENT RECORDS/RELEASE OF INFORMATION ON STUDENTS =====

This policy governs student education records and information release, addressing parent/guardian rights, record access procedures, amendment processes, and third-party disclosure circumstances.

KEY DEFINITIONS AND SCOPE

Student education records include identifying data; academic work completed; level of achievement (grades, standardized achievement test scores); attendance data; and scores on standardized intelligence, aptitude, and psychological tests. Records maintained by law enforcement units are excluded from this policy's protections.

ACCESS RIGHTS

Parents/guardians may inspect student records. Students age 18+ (eligible students) may access their own records, though parents of tax dependents retain access rights.

Access Procedures: records must be examined in the presence of the principal or designee. Original records remain in District/school buildings. One copy is provided within a reasonable time at a cost not to exceed 25 cents per page. Test protocols and completed test instruments cannot be copied due to copyright/integrity concerns.

RECORD AMENDMENT PROCESS

Initial Request: written amendment requests go to the building principal within 10 school days of the date that the student records were first examined.

Appeal Levels: a principal denial may be appealed to the assistant superintendent (10 days); an assistant superintendent denial may be followed by a formal hearing request to the Superintendent (10 days).

Formal Hearing Standards: the hearing is conducted within 15 school days; the parent/student may be assisted or represented by an individual of their choice at their own expense, including an attorney.

Outcome Rights: parents/students may place explanatory statements in records; these statements must be disclosed whenever the record is shared.

DISCLOSURE WITHOUT CONSENT

Permitted to District Officials: disclosure is allowed to employees with a legitimate educational interest when necessary for job duties, within an official business context, relevant to student-related tasks.

Third-Party Recipients: other schools/districts where the student enrolls; federal/state education authorities; financial aid processors; accrediting organizations; researchers and study organizations; and juvenile justice officials (with confidentiality certification).

Emergency Exception: disclosure is permitted in connection with an emergency, if knowledge of the information disclosed is necessary to protect the health or safety of the student or other individuals.

DIRECTORY INFORMATION

Disclosable Without Consent: student name, photograph, audio/video recordings, major field of study, grade level, athletic participation, weight/height of athletes, attendance dates, honors/awards, and previous institutions attended.

Protected Directory Elements: telephone numbers and addresses require express written permission and cannot be released without written consent.

Opt-Out Deadline: parents must submit written refusal no later than September 1.

SPECIAL DISCLOSURE PROVISIONS

Military Recruiting: names, addresses, and home telephone numbers of secondary students will be released to military recruiting officers within ninety (90) days unless the student objects in writing.

Medicaid: directory information (name, date of birth, gender) is released to the Colorado Health Care Policy and Financing agency; non-directory information requires annual written consent.

Alumni Directories: may include name, activities, sports participation, attendance dates, degrees, awards, and addresses of former students; current students' contact information is protected.

ANNUAL NOTIFICATIONS AND COMPLAINTS

Districts must notify families annually of their rights. Complaints regarding Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act violations may be submitted to the Family Policy Compliance Office, U.S. Department of Education.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

20 U.S.C. 1232g (FERPA); 34 C.F.R. 99.1 et seq.; Colorado Revised Statutes 19-1-303/304, 22-32-109.1(6), 22-32-109.3, and 24-72-204(3)(d)&(e).

===== POLICY JRCB — PRIVACY AND PROTECTION OF CONFIDENTIAL STUDENT RECORD INFORMATION =====

The district governs student record privacy through federal FERPA, Colorado's Student Data Transparency and Security Act, and related policies. "Confidential PII" refers to personally identifiable information within student education records.

KEY DEFINITIONS

Confidential Student Education Records comprise materials in any format that: (1) contain student-related information; (2) are maintained by district officials; and (3) are not classified as directory information.

Personally Identifiable Information includes data linked or linkable to a specific student enabling community identification, encompassing names, family member identifiers, addresses, social security numbers, student IDs, biometric records, and indirect identifiers like birth dates or maiden names.

Third Party means any entity or non-district employee.

AUTHORIZED ACCESS AND SHARING

Access to Confidential PII, and the collection and sharing of Confidential PII, is only authorized in accordance with governing law and District Policies CL and JRA/JRC.

ELECTRONIC DATA SECURITY STANDARDS

District computers, devices, email, and electronically stored data require password protection with complexity standards set by the information technology executive director. E-mail between District accounts shall be encrypted in transmission and at rest. Procedures enabling encryption between district and third-party accounts must exist. The operations executive director implements physical security, including building/data center access controls and video surveillance. The technology executive director implements access logging by device/location, penetration testing, vulnerability detection tools, breach response, and notification procedures. Compliance monitoring with policies EHA, GBEE, and JS is required. Staff must report suspected unauthorized access or data breaches.

ELECTRONIC DATA SECURITY AUDITS

Annual audits assess district equipment, software, and procedures protecting Confidential PII. The technology executive director submits confidential reports to the superintendent, with board distribution as directed.

DATA RETENTION AND DESTRUCTION

Records retention follows legal requirements and Policy EHB, with subsequent destruction per law and administrative procedures.

DISTRICT STAFF TRAINING

The district conducts periodic staff education on privacy obligations involving Confidential PII, including district technology, online services, and mobile applications.

SCHOOL SERVICE CONTRACT PROVIDER ACCOUNTABILITY

Contracts with school service contract providers committing a material breach involving misuse or unauthorized disclosure of Confidential PII face termination and future disqualification. Providers may request a hearing under Board Policy GP 3.12.

PARENT/GUARDIAN COMPLAINT PROCEDURES

Parents/guardians believing the district failed Colorado Act compliance may file written complaints with the superintendent within 30 calendar days of learning relevant facts.

Required complaint contents: (1) complainant and student contact information; (2) detailed event description with dates, times, and witness names; (3) involved district employee identification; (4) harm description; and (5) supporting documentation.

The superintendent or designee renders a written determination within 30 days, investigating if necessary. Unsatisfied complainants may appeal to the Board under Policy GP 3.12.

===== POLICY JS — STUDENT USE OF DISTRICT INFORMATION TECHNOLOGY =====

CORE AUTHORIZATION AND SUSPENSION

The district permits student use of its technology exclusively for education-related purposes. The district may suspend or terminate access at any time it is in the District's best interest and retains authority to adjust usage limits and parameters through its chief technology officer.

DEFINITIONS

District information technology encompasses computers, devices, provisioned software, email, curriculum resources, and internet access. A district technology device (DTD) is a District-purchased tablet, camera, recorder, hand-held device, computer, laptop, system, network, or associated peripheral with installed software. A personal technology device (PTD) is a privately-owned portable device including cell phones, tablets, laptops, GPS devices, cameras, wearables, and recorders. Academic dishonesty means engaging in a practice in relation to schoolwork that does not meet the requirements through undisclosed material misrepresentation. Plagiarism means representing that work that is not the original creation of the student's is the student's own; it constitutes a form of academic dishonesty.

DEVICE ACCESS AND INSURANCE

Students may receive DTDs conditioned on insurance charges, with exemptions for free/reduced lunch-eligible students. Intentional or reckless damage may result in access restrictions.

PRIVACY RIGHTS

Students possess no expectation of privacy when using District information technology. The district reserves the right to monitor, inspect, copy, review, isolate, store and/or remove (at any time and without notice) all usage, including internet communications. Student-created content remains student-authored, but all material and information accessed/received/created through District information technology remains the property of the District.

SECURITY REQUIREMENTS

Students must guard passwords carefully and disclose them only to parents/caregivers; avoid sharing credentials unless authorized by technology support personnel; lock or log off unattended devices accessing confidential information; and password-protect DTDs and PTDs used for district access.

EMAIL POLICY

Deleted emails remain accessible through the "deleted items" folder. The Information Technology Department may purge student email annually unless otherwise required by law or District policy.

INTERNET ACCESS

The district implements technology protection measures that block or filter internet material that is obscene, child pornography or otherwise harmful to minors. Teachers must instruct students on internet safety before allowing access. Students encountering inappropriate material must immediately back out of the site and notify a District employee.

HARDWARE AND SOFTWARE MODIFICATIONS

Students cannot connect hardware to district devices or modify or circumvent the operating conditions set by the Information Technology Department without express authorization.

ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE

The district acknowledges AI may enhance learning. Students may use AI tools only when clearly stated in the assignment or specified by the teacher. Citations of AI use are required. Unauthorized AI use may constitute plagiarism and academic dishonesty.

Students must recognize that AI tools are prone to "hallucinations," false answers/information, or outdated, misleading and/or biased information, and should verify results using reliable sources.

Critical restriction: students should not upload or input any personal, confidential, proprietary, or sensitive information into any AI tool accessed through District information technology, including passwords, personal information, images, and financial account numbers.

PROHIBITED USES

Students cannot use district technology to generate, send, receive, or store communications containing: content threatening safety; sexually-oriented or pornographic material; profanity directed at persons or classifications; violence advocacy or unlawful acts; information about weapons or controlled substances; harassment, bullying, threats, defamation, or violence against persons; plagiarism or academic dishonesty; privacy violations or confidentiality breaches; impersonation or anonymous proxy transmission; unauthorized access to district systems; unlicensed software or data downloads; violations of federal, state, or local law; policy violations; or interference with district operations or educational opportunities.

CONSEQUENCES

Violations result in potential suspension, modification, or revocation of use privileges, detention, and suspension or expulsion from school.

===== POLICY JT — YOUTH SUICIDE PREVENTION =====

The policy establishes the District's commitment to suicide prevention. The District recognizes that the physical and mental health of the entire school community are integral components of student outcomes and acknowledges suicide as a leading cause of youth death. The policy emphasizes the school's role in creating a supportive environment and recognizes employee responsibility to follow protocols.

RESOURCE AVAILABILITY

Resources that support suicide prevention and emotional wellness efforts will be maintained on the District's website and made publicly available.

EMPLOYEE OBLIGATIONS

District employees must be equipped with practical skills and remain alert to warning signs. Those aware of students exhibiting suicidal thoughts must follow current District protocols, typically coordinating with mental health special services providers.

MENTAL HEALTH PROVIDER RESPONSIBILITIES

Mental health specialists should conduct suicide risk screenings, contact parents/caregivers, and share available resources within District and community settings, including collaboration with crisis resources while complying with Policy JRA/JRC regarding student records.
