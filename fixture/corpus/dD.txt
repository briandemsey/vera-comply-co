===== POLICY DBAA — General Fund Balances =====

Adopted March 26, 2013; revised June 10, 2014.

POLICY OBJECTIVE
The district aims to maintain appropriate fund balances within the General Fund to maximize resources while complying with governing law, mitigating financial risk, and ensuring financial stability.

FUND BALANCE CATEGORIES (GASB STATEMENT NO. 54)
Nonspendable: amounts unable to be spent due to form or legal requirements (e.g., inventories, endowment principal).
Restricted: amounts spent only for specific purposes stipulated by the constitution or external resource providers.
Committed: amounts designated by formal Board action prior to fiscal year end (June 30); actual amounts may be determined later.
Assigned: amounts intended to be used by the District for specific purposes without meeting restricted/committed criteria; the superintendent or designee may assign funds.
Unassigned: the residual fund category comprising all spendable amounts not in other categories; may be used for any legal purpose.

FUND BALANCE USAGE PRIORITY
Restricted funds are spent first when applicable. Among unrestricted categories, committed funds are used before assigned, and assigned before unassigned. Within unassigned balances, reserves precede contingency reserves.

RESERVE REQUIREMENTS
The district shall strive to maintain a reserve amount between 3% and 5% of each fiscal year's expenditures. If reserves fall below 3%, shortages must be replenished within one year (for shortfalls of 0-1%) or two years (for shortfalls of 1-3%) through expenditure cuts or revenue increases. Excess reserves exceeding 5% may fund nonrecurring expenditures that require no future maintenance costs.

IMPLEMENTATION AND REVIEW
The Financial Services Department develops implementation guidelines and conducts annual policy reviews with recommendations to the Board.

C.R.S. 22-44-101 to -304.

===== POLICY DFA-DFAA — Revenues from Investment/Use of Surplus Funds =====

Adopted April 1972; revised April 1977, May 1982, April 1988, August 1994, May 22, 1995, August 11, 1997, October 14, 2002, June 28, 2004, February 28, 2012.

SCOPE
This policy applies to all Poudre School District financial assets and funds over which the District exercises control, as documented in the Comprehensive Annual Financial Report.

INVESTMENT OBJECTIVES
District funds not immediately needed shall be invested by Board-authorized individuals to accomplish four primary goals: (1) compliance with governing law and Board direction; (2) protection of financial assets; (3) maintaining liquidity for timely obligation payment; and (4) earning a competitive market rate of return from legally permitted investments while respecting risk constraints and cash flow needs.

INVESTMENT PRINCIPLES
Diversification: investments must be sufficiently diverse to protect against the risk of loss from over-concentration in specific maturities, issues, classes, or institutions.
Liquidity: investments must remain sufficiently liquid to allow continuity of the District's fiscal operations without emergency borrowing. The controller shall maintain updated cash-flow projections.
Pooling: all unrestricted moneys may be pooled into a single investment account with separate accounting. Income distribution follows governing law as determined by the executive director of finance or Board direction.

AUTHORITY AND RESPONSIBILITIES
Executive Director of Finance: holds overall responsibility for District investment decisions and transactions, including wire and electronic fund transfers. This individual also serves as Board Treasurer and shall consult independent investment advisors as needed.
Authorized Personnel: beyond the executive director of finance, the following employees may make investment decisions and conduct transactions: controller; primary accountant (designated in writing by executive director); backup accountant (designated in writing by executive director). Primary and backup accountants must be reported to the Board at the beginning of the fiscal year and whenever changed.
Broker/Dealer Standards: authorized broker/dealers must maintain a minimum net capital of $10,000,000 and a ten-year history of services to governmental entities. The executive director and investment advisors maintain lists of eligible brokers with current financial statements.

EMPLOYEE CONDUCT AND FIDUCIARY DUTIES
Employees must operate under Colorado law's fiduciary standard, exercising the judgment and care under the circumstances then prevailing that persons of prudence, discretion and intelligence exercise in the management of the property of another.
Prohibited Activities: employees shall avoid personal business activity conflicting with proper investment program execution or impairing impartial decision-making.
Disclosure Requirements: employees must disclose to the executive director, superintendent, and Board any material financial interests in financial institutions conducting District business or under consideration, plus personal financial positions potentially affected by portfolio performance.
Bonding and Insurance: investment decision-makers must be bonded, with District insurance against their misfeasance.
Training: all authorized employees shall receive education and training necessary to ensure their proficiency as fiduciaries.

INTERNAL CONTROLS
The District maintains controls including: (1) written documentation of all transactions; (2) at least two authorized employees, as designated in this policy, signing off in advance on investments; and (3) periodic internal auditor review. The executive director shall ensure annual policy review with revisions submitted to the Board.

REPORTING AND ACCOUNTABILITY
Accounting Standards: investments follow generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP) and Governmental Accounting Standards Board standards, included in annual external audits.
Monthly Reports: the controller prepares performance statements showing earnings and rate of return for each investment in the current month and for the term of each investment to date.
Public Posting: annual audits and monthly reports are posted online and maintained in fiscal records.
Board Notification: the Board receives investment, earnings, and rate-of-return updates on no less than a quarterly basis.

LEGAL REFERENCES
C.R.S. 11-10.5-101 et seq.; 11-47-101 et seq.; 11-60-101 et seq.; 15-1-304; 22-32-107; 22-40-105; 24-75-601 et seq.; 24-75-701 et seq.

CROSS REFERENCES
DG (Depository of Funds); DH (Bonded Employees and Officers); GBEA (Staff Ethics/Conflict of Interest).

===== POLICY DIE — Audits/Financial Monitoring =====

ANNUAL AUDIT REQUIREMENT
The district must conduct annual audits of all funds and accounts following fiscal year close, as required by state law.

The Board appoints an independent auditor who is licensed to practice in Colorado and knowledgeable in government accounting. This auditor must also audit the district's activities accounts for Board reporting.

The Board reserves discretion to request audits at more frequent intervals.

===== POLICY DIEA-R — Internal Audit Services =====

Adopted August 13, 2012; revised July 25, 2016.

INTERNAL AUDITOR
The District may employ an internal auditor reporting to the executive director of finance with oversight from the District Audit Committee. The auditor reports directly to the finance director and, as requested, to the controller, superintendent, and Audit Committee. On matters presenting impropriety or conflict concerns, the internal auditor reports to the superintendent or Board President.

PURPOSE
The internal auditor assists in resource safeguarding by promoting an economical, efficient and effective business environment based on sound and practical internal controls. Services aim to evaluate organizational processes systematically and provide independent and objective insight, analysis and recommendations for constructive change and risk management. The philosophy emphasizes adding value to District operations and controls.

PROFESSIONAL STANDARDS
The internal auditor operates under The Institute of Internal Auditors' Code of Ethics and International Standards for the Professional Practice of Internal Auditing and adheres to applicable Practice Advisories.

ACCESS TO INFORMATION
The auditor receives full, free, timely and unrestricted access to any and all District records, physical properties and personnel relevant to functions under review. All employees must cooperate. Documents receive confidential handling. Administrators and Finance staff must inform appropriate officials in a timely manner of any significant breaches affecting compliance systems related to accounting and financial activities.

INDEPENDENCE
The auditor remains free of improper influence from District employees regarding audit selection, procedures, frequency, timing, or content. The auditor has no operational responsibility over reviewed activities and shall not develop systems, install procedures, or prepare records normally subject to audit.

AUDIT SCOPE
Services focus on activities affecting compliance with accounting and financial standards, regulations, procedures and policies, including: reviewing financial information reliability, operating systems, and measurement/reporting mechanisms; examining compliance systems regarding governing laws and District policies; reviewing asset safeguarding means and verifying asset existence; recommending internal control improvements; reviewing external auditor performance and coordination; assisting in investigations of suspected law or policy violations; informing leadership of emerging internal audit trends; and reviewing employee compliance with significant financial policy requirements. Matters exclusively involving non-financial operations fall outside current scope.

AUDIT PLANNING
The finance director and controller collaborate with the internal auditor to develop an annual plan submitted to the Audit Committee. Planning uses a risk-based methodology prioritizing the financial audit universe. Significant deviations require communication to the finance director and Audit Committee.

REPORTING
Periodic internal audit reports go to the finance director, with copies to the Audit Committee and external auditors. Reports include locations visited, audit ratings, significant findings, employee responses, and corrective actions with completion timelines. The finance director and controller collaborate on follow-up, with the Audit Committee providing input. Issues remain "open" until all parties agree on resolution.

PERIODIC ASSESSMENT
The finance director, controller, internal auditor, and Audit Committee periodically assess whether services adequately fulfill regulatory purposes, recommending necessary revisions to the superintendent.

The policy aims to provide for fair and equitable treatment of all persons or entities involved in purchasing, maximize the value of District funds, standardize the purchasing procedures, and foster full and open competition.

VENDOR SELECTION CRITERIA
Employees must strive to obtain the best possible terms and conditions, price, quality, warranty/guarantee, support and overall value.

COOPERATIVE PURCHASING
The District may enter cooperative agreements with governmental entities when deemed to be in the best interest of the District and procedures are compatible with District policies.

GRANT PURCHASES
Grant-funded purchases may require special management, accounting or procedures with Finance Department coordination for compliance.

PUBLIC RECORDS ACCESS
Requests regarding purchase records follow the Colorado Open Records Act (CORA).

===== POLICY DJA — Purchasing Authority, Methods, and Thresholds =====

Last revised May 13, 2025 (effective July 1, 2025).

PURCHASING AUTHORITY
The Board retains ultimate authority over all District purchases, with delegated authority based on purchase value: $250,000 or more requires prior Board approval; under $250,000 may be approved by the superintendent or chief finance officer if funds are budgeted and the purchase aligns with law and policy; under $10,000 may be approved via purchasing card (p-card) by Finance Department authorized employees. The chief finance officer may delegate authority to Finance Department employees through written documentation annually. District administrators may veto any employee purchase they supervise.

PURCHASING METHODS
Documented Price Quotes: requires soliciting written quotes from at least three (3) qualified vendors, with the Finance Department maintaining records. The Finance Department may approve purchases with fewer than three quotes if reasonable attempts were made.
Competitive Bidding: administered by the Finance Department, requiring detailed specifications, written terms/conditions, procedures, solicitation of at least three (3) qualified bidders, and a public/electronic bid opening.
Competitive Proposals: appropriate for complex purchases, requiring objectives/descriptions, evaluation procedures, solicitation from at least three qualified organizations, potential interviews by two District officials (one from Finance), and a written contract.

PURCHASING THRESHOLDS
Under $10,000: no competitive method required.
$10,000-$100,000: minimum three documented price quotes required.
$100,000+: competitive bidding or proposal process required.
Purchases cannot be divided to circumvent these methods.

EXCEPTIONS
Educational Equipment: superintendent or assistant superintendent may authorize based on a written need determination without competition.
Cooperative Purchasing: chief finance officer may authorize joint purchasing arrangements with other agencies.
Sole Source Items: chief finance officer has discretion to approve unique goods/services from single vendors with sufficient documentation.
Standardization/Compatibility: Finance Department develops guidelines for system interoperability justifications.
Conferences/Speakers: District administrator may authorize based on a written need determination.
District's Best Interests: superintendent or chief finance officer designee may waive methods if competition would result in a substantially higher cost or adversely affect the District.

EMERGENCIES
Defined as imminent threats to health, welfare, safety, property, or creating excessive financial risk/liability exposure. Designated administrators may make immediate purchases without competitive requirements, submitting written explanation to the Finance Department afterward. The chief finance officer reviews all emergency purchases and reports those exceeding $250,000 to the Board.

GRIEVANCES
Vendors may protest within ten (10) business days of the challenged action. Investigation leads to an executive director decision, appealable to the chief finance officer. The superintendent's decision is final for purchases under $250,000; the Board may hear appeals for $250,000+ purchases.

===== POLICY DJB — Contracting =====

Adopted May 13, 2025; effective July 1, 2025.

OVERVIEW
The policy governs all District contracts for goods and services procurement regardless of cost. A contract is mandated for services that may create liability for the District unless exempted by administrative guidelines approved by legal counsel. Purchases of $10,000 or more require either a purchase order with standard written terms or an executed contract per this policy.

DEFINITIONS
Contract: an agreement between the District and one or more parties that creates mutual obligations that are enforceable or otherwise recognizable at law.
Cooperative Purchasing Agreement: a process allowing the District to purchase goods/services at pre-negotiated rates using existing contracts awarded by other public agencies.
District-Issued Contract: an agreement generated by the District for specific services/goods, requiring generation or minimum review/approval by Finance or legal counsel.
Material Change: a modification to a standard District template or standard written terms and conditions that alters the legal obligations of a party.
Service: work by non-District employees providing specialized knowledge/skills/expertise, typically intangible.
Standard District Template: contracts reviewed/approved by legal counsel for construction, service agreements, software purchases, community partnerships, and other services.
Standard Written Terms and Conditions: standard contract approved by legal counsel for goods purchases (excluding software).
Vendor Contract: vendor-issued agreements requiring Finance or legal counsel review before execution.

CONTRACT EXECUTION AND APPROVAL
The District prioritizes standard written terms, standard templates, or District-issued contracts. Exceptions require Finance or legal counsel approval. Standard District templates bypass legal counsel review unless material changes are requested. Cooperative purchasing agreements may be executed if in the District's best interest and compliant with District policies.

REQUIREMENTS FOR WRITTEN CONTRACTS
Criminal Background Check Provision: all written contracts must require a criminal background check for any person providing direct services to students under the contract, including but not limited to instruction, physical, mental, and social health supports, transportation or food services as required by law. The contracting entity bears background check costs.
Public Works Contingency: construction/design contracts must include provisions making District obligations contingent on appropriated/budgeted funds per C.R.S. § 24-91-103.6.
Insurance Requirements: all written contracts must include insurance requirements reviewed and approved by risk management.

CONTRACT APPROVAL AUTHORITY
Contracts require signatures from the appropriate employee with purchasing authority or the Board's presiding officer. The Board may delegate negotiation/execution to specified staff upon purchase amount approval.

APPROVAL THRESHOLDS (TABLE)
No cost: Principal/Manager/Director and Executive Director/Cabinet Member sign.
Under $10,000: Principal/Manager/Director and Executive Director/Cabinet Member sign.
$10,000-$100,000: Principal/Manager/Director, Executive Director/Cabinet Member, and Legal Counsel sign.
$100,000-$250,000: Principal/Manager/Director, Executive Director/Cabinet Member, Legal Counsel, and Finance Designee sign.
$250,000+: Principal/Manager/Director, Executive Director/Cabinet Member, Legal Counsel, and Finance Designee sign; Board Presiding Officer signature required unless Board delegates signing authority.
District receiving payment: Principal/Manager/Director and Executive Director/Cabinet Member sign; Finance Designee required when federal grant funding is involved or District receives payment (excluding donations).
Legal Counsel approval is required for non-standard templates or high-risk contracts, and for standard templates with material changes.
